# When to add a Charter / Mandate layer

A Charter (or Mandate, or Terms of Reference) is a board-level document that **establishes a function** — its purpose, scope, authority, governance, and dissolution conditions.

General Bank in this worked example does **not** maintain a separate Charter. The ISMS Charter content is held within [POL-00 Section 4](../01-policies/00-information-security-policy.md) — "ISMS establishment". This is appropriate for a Maturity-3 bank where the master policy is itself board-approved.

---

## When a separate Charter earns its keep

Add a separate Charter when **at least one** of the following is true:

| Trigger | Why a Charter helps |
|---|---|
| The function's governance is materially more complex than a policy can capture | E.g., a Cyber Defence function with reporting lines into both Risk and Operations, with its own committee structure. |
| The Board has explicitly asked for terms of reference for the function | Typical post-IPO, post-merger, or post-regulatory-escalation. The Board wants a discrete document it can reference in minutes. |
| Multiple policies share a common governance umbrella that needs to be stated once | E.g., a Risk Charter that establishes the three-line model is referenced by InfoSec, Operational Risk, and Compliance policies. |
| There is a planned dissolution or transition condition | E.g., a Security Transformation Programme established for a defined period, with sunset criteria. |

## When a Charter does **not** earn its keep

- When it would simply duplicate content already in the master policy.
- When the organisation has not yet stabilised the underlying policies — Charters built on shifting policies are written then rewritten.
- When the Board is comfortable with the master policy as the governance document.

## If you add one

Recommended structure:

1. **Establishment** — by what authority, when.
2. **Purpose** — why this function exists.
3. **Scope** — what is in and out of remit.
4. **Authority** — what the function can decide; what is reserved to higher authority.
5. **Governance** — committee structure, reporting lines, escalation.
6. **Membership / role** — who holds the function (where applicable).
7. **Operating principles** — high-level principles below which the supporting policies operate.
8. **Review** — cadence and trigger for review of the Charter itself.
9. **Dissolution** — conditions and authority for retirement.

Cadence: **rarely reviewed** (every 2–3 years, or on material change). If a Charter is reviewed annually with changes, it is doing the job of a policy.
